Latin-derived -atus Adjectives

Many Latin verbs formed perfect participles ending in -atus. 
In Inglish, adjectives derived from these forms follow systematic patterns that maintain their etymological connections.


Pattern A: -âte → -atif
ta relâte → relatif
ta negâte → negatif


Pattern B: -de → -ssif
ta decîde → decîssif
ta explôde → explôssif
ta erôde → erôssif
ta corode → corôssif


Pattern C: -cte → -ctif
ta acte → actif
to infecte → infectif
ta protecte → protectif


Pattern D: -ete/-aite → -aitif/-atif/-âtif
ta demonstrete → demonstraitif
ta óperaite → ópratif
ta coóperaite → coópratif
ta m̃utete → m̃utâtif
ta pêmiete → pêmiaitif


Pattern E: -mitte → -missif
to admitte → admissif
ta permitte → permissif
ta submitte → submissif


Irregular Formations:
ta criâte → criâtif
to equâte → equâtif


Pronunciation:
The -if ending is pronounced /ɪv/, like English -ive
Stress typically falls on the syllable before the -if suffix
