## Why Reform?

English stands as a testament to the complex evolution of language. When St. Augustine introduced the Latin alphabet in 597 AD, English began its transition from Germanic runes to Latin letters. The Norman Conquest of 1066 deepened this transformation, blending local vernaculars with Norman French to create a language that bridged European traditions. Crucially, during this period, English pronunciation and the Latin alphabet were closely aligned, a harmony shared with neighboring continental languages.

However, the Great Vowel Shift, spanning the 15th to 18th centuries, dramatically altered spoken English without updating its written form. This historic divorce of sound from letter created a persistent mismatch between spelling and pronunciation that defines the language to this day.

Today, as the global lingua anglica, English seamlessly absorbs loanwords without adapting their spellings to fit native phonetic patterns. While this historical fidelity preserves incredible etymological depth, it heavily burdens language acquisition. Both native speakers and global learners are forced to rely on rote memorization to navigate the unpredictable orthography—a reality that underscores the urgent need for structural reform.

True orthographic reform requires a nuanced approach: one that respects etymological history while accurately mapping contemporary sounds. To achieve this, this project anchors itself to the Midwestern accent of the United States. Known for its clarity and status as the standard in national broadcasting, this accent provides the stable, recognizable phonetic baseline required to repair the damage of the Great Vowel Shift.

To bridge the gap between historical spelling and modern speech, this reform draws direct inspiration from French, Spanish, and Portuguese orthography. As descendants of Latin, these Romance languages experienced their own massive phonetic shifts, yet they successfully evolved their spelling conventions to match. Because these are also major languages of the Americas, regions in constant linguistic exchange with English, aligning our spelling mechanics with theirs creates a highly intuitive, globally recognizable system.

By balancing historical richness with mechanical practicality, a phonologically consistent English becomes vastly more accessible. This approach eases the learning curve for native speakers and global learners alike, particularly those from Romance language backgrounds, fostering a stronger, more logical communication bridge across our communities.
