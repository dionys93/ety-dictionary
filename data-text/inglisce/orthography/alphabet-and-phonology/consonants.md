# Consonants in Inglisce

Inglisce reorganizes the English consonant inventory to achieve absolute phonetic predictability while preserving vital etymological markers. Ambiguous digraphs (like *ch*) are strictly regulated or replaced, and the system relies on structured diacritics to indicate predictable shifts like assibilation or palatalization.

## 2. Structural and Etymological Rules

### The F / PH / FH System (/f/ preservation)
Inglisce employs a layered system for the `/f/` sound to preserve morphology and prevent visual collisions between distinct roots.
* **F (Default):** Used in most positions (*farme*, *aftre*).
* **FH (Historical *gh*):** When modern English uses the chaotic `-gh` to represent `/f/`, Inglisce maps it to `-fh` at the end of a word (*rough* → **rofh**). If a suffix is added, it simplifies to **f** (*laughter* → **lafture**).
* **PH (Collision & Root Preservation):** * Medial `-ph-` is always retained (*filosophie*, *symphonie*).
  * Initial `ph-` is collapsed to `f-` (*fótograph*) **unless** doing so creates a collision with an existing word (e.g., *phone* and *prophet* retain the `ph`).
  * If `ph` precedes `r`, it immediately simplifies to `fr` (*phrase* → **frâse**, *zephyr* → **zefre** ).

### The G and GN Rules
The silent 'g' and historical 'k' clusters in English are handled strictly by their etymological origins, consolidating spelling and preserving root families:

* **The Germanic ⟨gn-⟩ Consolidation:** In native Germanic words, the silent initial cluster is standardized to **gn-** (pronounced `/n/`). 
  * It preserves original Germanic `gn-` roots (e.g., *gnaw* → **gnahe**, *gnat* → **gnate**, *gnash* → **gnașe**).
  * It actively replaces the Modern English `kn-` cluster, pulling those words into the same consistent orthographic rule (e.g., *knit* → **gnite**, *knowledge* → **gnollage**, *acknowledge* → **acgnollage**).
  * *Exception:* The word *knight* drops the silent onset entirely, simplifying to **nîhte**.

* **Latin & French (Preserved & Sounded):** In words of Romance origin, the 'g' is preserved to anchor the vowel shift and maintain the root for derivations.
  * **-ign** = `/aɪn/` (e.g., *sign* → **signe**, *vine* → **vigne**, *wine* → **uigne**). When a suffix is added, the hard 'g' usually sounds (e.g., **signațure**).
  * **-aign / -eigne** = `/eɪn/` (e.g., *reign* → **reigne**, *campaign* → **campaign**).
  * **The French Palatal Nasal (gn = /nj/):** While the **ñ** diacritic is the standard marker for the palatalized 'n', specific Romance imports preserve the **gn** digraph to represent that exact same `/nj/` sound, maintaining their historical visual identity (e.g., *onion* → **ognon**, *vineyard* → **vignard**).
  * **Aesthetic Re-addition:** Inglisce sometimes restores a Latin 'g' that Modern English lost, completing the visual root family (e.g., *complain* → **complaigne**, matching *complaignant*).

### The Letter Thorn ( Ћ / þ )
Inglisce restores the letter Thorn to replace the Modern English `th` digraph. 
* **Voiced (/ð/):** Used in grammatical function words (*þe*, *þis*, *þat*) and typically between two vowels (*brieþe*, *cloaþe*).
* **Voiceless (/θ/):** Used in standard content words (*braiþ*, *þiorie*) and non-intervocalic positions.
