Modern English -gh from Middle English

Words with /aɪ/ sound:
   the sight -> þe sîhte /saɪt/
   the sigh -> þe syhe /saɪ/
   the fright -> þe fryhte /fraɪt/
   the flight -> þe flyhte /flaɪt/
   the fight -> þe foihte /faɪt/
   the knight -> þe nîhte /naɪt/
   sleight -> slyhte /slaɪt/
   slight -> slîhte /slaɪt/
   the height -> þe hoihte /haɪt/
   the night -> þe naihte /naɪt/ (Spelling influenced by Old English "næht")

Words with /eɪ/ sound:
   the weight -> þe ueihte /weɪt/
   the freight -> þe freihte /freɪt/

Words where -gh is pronounced as /f/:
   cough -> coffe (n), to coffe (v)
   enough -> enofh
   laugh -> lafhe (n), to lafhe (v)
   rough -> rofh (adj), to rofane (v)
   slough -> slofh (n), to slofhe (v)
   tough -> tofh (adj), to tofane (v)


Words where "gh" is dropped entirely:
   plough/plow -> plôe
   slough -> slôe
   thorough -> þouro
   though, although -> þo, alþó
   through -> þru


Verbs with irregular past tense in -aught/-ought:
Inglish simplifies these patterns by using -oht consistently for all verbs in this category, making the system more regular while preserving the connection between forms:
   to catch, caught -> to caic̄e, cauht
   to teach, taught -> to tiec̄e, toht
   to think, thought -> to þinche, þoht
   to seek, sought -> to síche, soht
   to fight, fought -> to foihte, foht
   to buy, bought -> to byhe, boht
