# Verbalizers (Verb-Forming Suffixes)

---

## 1. The ⟨-ifae / -fae⟩ Suffix (Modern English *-ify / -fy*)

In Modern English, the suffix **-fy** or **-ify** is used to form verbs meaning "to make" or "to cause to be." It descends from the Latin roots *-facere* and *-ficare*, passing through the Old French *-fier*. 

**Inglisce** standardizes this verbalizer to **-ifae** or **-fae**. 

### The ⟨-fae⟩ Shift (Unpacking)
Verbs ending in **-fae** follow a strictly predictable pattern. They conjugate smoothly using the base verb stem, but "unpack" into their heavier, historical Latinate structures when deriving into abstract nouns or adjectives. 

* **Active Conjugations:** to **jostifae**, jostifaes, jostifaed, jostifyhing

When deriving into a noun or an adjective, the base reaches back to its Latin *-ficatio* or *-abilis* roots, utilizing the **-ficâcion** (or related structural variants) and **-fîable** endings.

| Inglisce Verb | Unpacked Derivation (Noun / Adjective) |
| :--- | :--- |
| to **jostifae** | **jostificâcion**, **jostifîable** |
| to **satisfae** | **satisfaccion** *(irregular root)* |
| to **amplifae** | **amplificâcion** |
| to **signifae** | **significance** *(irregular root)* |
| to **mortifae** | **mortificâcion** |

## 2. The ⟨-yse⟩ Suffix (Modern English *-ize / -ise*)

The suffix **-ize** is widely used to create verbs indicating transformation, organization, or action (meaning "to make" or "subject to a process"). It originates from the Greek *-ίζειν* (*-izein*), was adopted into Latin as *-izare*, and passed into Old French as *-iser*.

While Modern English is split between the British *-ise* and the American *-ize*, **Inglisce** draws inspiration from the Greek upsilon and the Old French forms, standardizing the verb suffix strictly to **-yse**. 

When these verbs derive into abstract nouns, the spelling shifts to the **-isâcion** ending to reflect the historical Latin *-izatio*.

| Modern English | Root Meaning | Inglisce Verb | Inglisce Abstract Noun |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **realize** | From *real* (to make real). | to **rialyse** | **þa rialisâcion** |
| **organize** | From *organ* (to arrange). | to **organyse** | **þi organisâcion** |
| **modernize** | From *modern* (to make modern). | to **modernyse** | **þa modernisâcion** |
| **authorize** | From *authority* (to permit). | to **auþoryse** | **þi auþorisâcion** |

## 3. The Romance Verb Suffix ⟨-ișe⟩ (Modern English *-ish*)

In Modern English, the suffix **-ish** presents a massive orthographic collision. The language uses the exact same spelling for native Germanic adjectives (like *British* or *childish*) and imported French verbs (like *finish* or *abolish*). 

Etymologically, this verb suffix has absolutely no connection to the Germanic word-builder. It originates from the Latin inchoative infix *-iscere*, which evolved into the Old French *-iss-* marker used to conjugate specific verbs (e.g., *ils finissent*). When English absorbed these extended verb stems (*feniss-*, *aboliss-*), the pronunciation shifted to the fricative `/ʃ/`. Lazy English scribes simply mashed it into the exact same orthographic box as the Germanic adjective.

**Inglisce** repairs this collision by visually separating the two etymologies based on their grammatical function. While Germanic adjectives take the ancestral **-isce**, Romance verbs adopt the distinct **-ișe**, utilizing the **ș** (s-comma) to map the `/ʃ/` sound. This perfectly preserves the phonetic reality while isolating the Romance verb class.

### 1. The Standard Verb Overhaul (-ișe)
For the vast majority of imported Romance verbs, the Modern English `-ish` is cleanly replaced by the Inglisce **-ișe** ending.

| Modern English | Old French Extended Stem | Inglisce Verb | 
| :--- | :--- | :--- | 
| **abolish** | *aboliss-* | to **abolișe** | 
| **admonish** | *amones-* | to **admonișe** | 
| **banish** | *baniss-* | to **banișe** | 
| **finish** | *feniss-* | to **finișe** | 
| **diminish** | *menuiss-* | to **diminișe** | 
| **publish** | *pupliss-* | to **publișe** | 

### 2. The Velar Glide Variation (-oișe)
For Romance verbs ending in *-guish* and *-quish*, the preceding `/w/` glide is standardized using the native vowel glide rules established in Inglisce. The clunky `gu-` and `qu-` clusters are stripped of their French interference and mapped using **o** to create the glide, resulting in the smooth, logical **-oișe** ending.

| Modern English | Etymological Root | Inglisce Verb | 
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **extinguish** | Latin *exstinguere* | to **extingoișe** |
| **vanquish** | Old French *venquiss-* | to **vencoișe** |
