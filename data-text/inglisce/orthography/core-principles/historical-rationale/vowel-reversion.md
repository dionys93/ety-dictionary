## The Philosophy of Vowel Reversion in Inglisce

Modern English orthography is often criticized as chaotic, but it is not without rules. Rather, it is a collision of multiple rule sets—Old English, Norman French, and Latin—frozen in time just before the most massive phonetic upheaval in the language's history: **The Great Vowel Shift (GVS).**

While the spoken language drifted for centuries—breaking stable long vowels into complex diphthongs and merging distinct sounds into single phonemes—the spelling remained stubbornly anchored to the 1400s. The result is a modern language where the written vowel sounds completely contradict their original Continental roots.

Inglisce does not attempt to reverse the Great Vowel Shift phonetically; people will continue to speak Modern English. Instead, Inglisce **reverses the orthographic damage** caused by the shift. 

### The Romance Scribe Protocol

To accomplish this, Inglisce adopts the perspective of a medieval Romance scribe tasked with standardizing a rapidly shifting vernacular. 

When Italian and French scribes inherited Classical Latin, they did not throw away the alphabet when the spoken language evolved. Instead, they built predictable orthographic bridges (like introducing `ch` or modifying vowel structures) to adapt the classical roots to the new phonetic reality. 

Inglisce applies this exact same protocol to Modern English:

1. **The Continental Baseline:** Inglisce assumes the historical, Continental vowel sound (where A is /ɑ/, E is /e/, and I is /i/) as the root baseline.
2. **Visual DNA Preservation:** The etymological and visual core of a word is preserved whenever possible. 
3. **Mechanical Modernization:** When modern pronunciation demands it, Inglisce applies strict, Romance-inspired diacritics and digraphs (like split digraphs or the `ai` mutation) to accurately capture the modern spoken sound without destroying the word's history.

### The Goal of Reversion

The goal of Vowel Reversion is not historical purism, but **phonetic transparency**. 

By applying a consistent set of mechanical rules to historical roots, Inglisce ensures that a reader can always predict how a word is pronounced simply by looking at it, while simultaneously understanding exactly where that word came from. 

We are not fighting the Great Vowel Shift; we are finally updating the spelling to accommodate it.
