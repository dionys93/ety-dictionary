## The Philosophy of Vowel Reversion

If the consonants of Modern English provide the structural skeleton of the language, the vowels are the source of its orthographic chaos. 

This chaos is largely the result of the Great Vowel Shift (GVS). Between the 15th and 18th centuries, the spoken vowels of English fractured and shifted, but the advent of the printing press froze the spelling in place. Today, the written vowels in English completely contradict their original Continental roots.

Inglisce does not attempt to reverse the Great Vowel Shift phonetically; people will continue to speak with modern pronunciation. Instead, Inglisce **reverses the orthographic damage**. 

### The Continental Baseline
To repair the vowel system, Inglisce establishes a strict Continental baseline. Rather than accepting the shifted, unpredictable sounds of Modern English as the default, it assumes the historical, classical values of the Latin alphabet as the phonetic foundation for any root word. 

In this system, the core vowels (like A, E, and I) behave as they historically did across Europe, rooting the English vocabulary back into its broader, continental context before any modern adjustments are made.

### The Romance Scribe Protocol
When a modern English (Midwestern) pronunciation deviates from that Continental baseline due to the Great Vowel Shift, Inglisce does not simply invent a new, unrecognizable spelling. 

Instead, it applies the "Romance Scribe Protocol." It utilizes predictable orthographic bridges—specifically diacritics (like the circumflex `^` and acute accent `´`) and targeted split digraphs—to accurately encode the modern spoken sound while preserving the etymological DNA of the word. 

* A word's history is preserved in its consonants and root vowels.
* Its modern pronunciation is mapped by its diacritics and suffixes.

The goal of Vowel Reversion is phonetic transparency: ensuring that a reader can always predict how a word is pronounced simply by looking at it, while simultaneously understanding exactly where that word came from.
