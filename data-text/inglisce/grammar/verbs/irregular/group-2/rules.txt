GROUP 2 IRREGULAR VERBS: CONJUGATION PATTERNS

These verbs historically formed their past tense through vowel changes (ablaut) in Old English strong verb classes.
In Inglisce, they preserve these irregular patterns through systematic vowel alternations and consonant clusters in the past tense.

PATTERN A: GUTTURAL PAST (-auht/-oht)
--------------------------------------
Verbs forming past with back vowels + guttural consonants: caiche, cique, tieche, foihte, bringe, byhe, þincue

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to caiche
- Simple Present: I caiche, sie caiches, uie caiche, þey caiche, you caiche
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I cauht, sie cauht, uie cauht, þey cauht, you cauht

- Infinitive: to cique
- Simple Present: I ciec, sie ciecs, uie cique, þey cique, you ciec/cique
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I çoht, sie çoht, uie çoht, þey çoht, you çoht

Key: Vowel shifts to au/o + ht cluster marks the past tense.

PATTERN B: DENTAL PAST WITH DIPHTHONGS (-ait/-aid/-aint)
---------------------------------------------------------
Verbs forming past with ai diphthong + dental consonants: miene, mete, lide, riede, lende

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to miene
- Simple Present: I miene, sie mienes, uie miene, þey miene, you miene
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I maint, sie maint, uie maint, þey maint, you maint

Key: Stem vowel becomes ai + t/d marks the past tense.

PATTERN C: LABIAL PAST (-aipt/-aift)
-------------------------------------
Verbs with stem + pt/ft clusters: quipe, sliepe, criepe, suiepe, uiepe, clievfe, lievfe

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to sliepe
- Simple Present: I sliepe, sie sliepes, uie sliepe, þey sliepe, you sliepe
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I slaipt, sie slaipt, uie slaipt, þey slaipt, you slaipt

Key: ie → ai shift + labial cluster marks the past tense.

PATTERN D: NASAL PAST (-aund/-ound)
------------------------------------
Verbs with î + nd cluster: bînde, fînde, grînde, uînde

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to fînde
- Simple Present: I fînde, sie fîndes, uie fînde, þey fînde, you fînde
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I faûnd, sie faûnd, uie faûnd, þey faûnd, you faûnd

Key: Long î → au + nd preserves the nasal ending.

PATTERN E: LATERAL/DENTAL PAST (-eld/-elt/-olt/-old)
-----------------------------------------------------
Verbs with l/lt/ld clusters: holde, selle, telle, fiele, diele, niele, smelle, spille, bylde

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to fiele
- Simple Present: I fiele, sie fieles, uie fiele, þey fiele, you fiele
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I felt, sie felt, uie felt, þey felt, you felt

Key: Vowel reduction/change + lateral consonant marks the past.

PATTERN F: SIMPLE DENTAL (-ent/-ed)
------------------------------------
Verbs adding simple -t or keeping -ed: bende, sende, spende, bêne, briede, fiede

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to sende
- Simple Present: I sende, sie sendes, uie sende, þey sende, you sende
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I sent, sie sent, uie sent, þey sent, you sent

Key: Minimal change - drops vowel before adding -t.

PATTERN G: STANDING VERBS (-ôd)
--------------------------------
Verbs of position/state: stande, ondrestande

Conjugation:
- Infinitive: to stande
- Simple Present: I stande, sie standes, uie stande, þey stande, you stande
- Simple Past/Past Participle: I stôd, sie stôd, uie stôd, þey stôd, you stôd

Key: Complete vowel replacement to ô + d.

SPECIAL CASES
-------------
- hyre → hêd - unique suppletive form
- sitte → sat, scite → sciat, spitte → spat - complete vowel alternation
- hângue → hong (intransitive) vs hângue → hângued (transitive) - transitivity distinction
- spinne → spon, uine → uon - unique -on endings

REGULAR SUFFIXES
----------------
All patterns use regular suffixes:
- 3rd person singular present: -s (caiches, mienes, sliepes)
- Present participle: -ing (caiching, miening, slieping)

ETYMOLOGY NOTE
--------------
These patterns preserve Old English strong verb classes where tense was marked by vowel gradation (ablaut) rather than suffixation.
The seven main patterns reflect different phonological environments: gutturals (Pattern A), dentals (B, F), labials (C), 
nasals (D), laterals (E), and special position verbs (G). This system maintains historical irregularity while organizing it 
into semi-predictable patterns based on phonological features.
