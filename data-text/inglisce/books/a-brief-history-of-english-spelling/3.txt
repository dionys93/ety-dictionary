Inglisce lòst and faund

Seortly aftre þe Norman Conquaist in 1066, Norman Frenc̃e replâced Inglisce as þe lânguage of govirnment and þe nóbils, 
but Inglisce aluays remained þe lânguage of þe common píple. 
Hôuevre Frenc̃e uords begane to bie used in Inglisce and þis has had a diepe and lasting effecte on þe lânguage, not liest þe spelling.

In þi end, Norman Frenc̃e uent into declîne aftre þe lòss of moast of Ingland's Frenc̃e lands. 
Ћen Inglisce (nôe Middle Inglisce) begane to bie adopted uance more for official and litterairy use. 
Ћis hapaned during þe 14þ cențurie, but þe process uas not complite untill abaut 1430.

Exampels of uêx in Middle Inglisce incloude "Sir Gawain & the Green Knight", 
"Langland's Piers Ploughman" and "Chaucer's Canterbury Tales".

Ћi êly 15þ cențurie sah attèmpts at standardysing Inglisce spelling. 
Ћe maign uan is gnone as þe "Chancery Standard", becase it uas used by þe Corte of Chancerie and oþre official bodis.

Hôuevre, þe nou sistem uas not consistent. It used boaþ Inglisce and Frenc̃e uays of spelling, 
uic̃ accôntes for meny of þe problems in modern Inglisce spelling. 
Alþó som uords of Frenc̃e origin uere rispelled to sute Inglisce spiec̃e, eg boeuf > beef, bataille > battle, compter > count, 
oþars uere not, eg table, double, centre. (Inglisce: biefe, battle, cônte, tâble, doble, centre)

Very êly Êly Modern Inglisce uêx incloude þe moralety plaie "Everyman" (lât 15þ cențurie) and Thomas Malory's "Le Morte d'Arthur" (as printed by Caxton in 1485).
