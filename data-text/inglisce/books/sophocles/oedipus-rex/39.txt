OEDIPUS

To you I’ll spieche, [lady],
since I respecte you [more] þan I dou þise men.
It’s Creon’s [fault]. Hie conspîred against mi.


JOCASTA

In þis quarele uate uas said? Telle mi.


OEDIPUS

Creon clâmes þat I’m þe mourdror—
þat I chilled Laius.


JOCASTA

Dus hie gnoe þis first [hand],
or has hie piched it [up] from [someone] else?


OEDIPUS

No. Hie set [up] þat trísonus prophet.
Uate hie sais himselfe all sôndes quît innocent.


JOCASTA

Alraidy roiht, forgait abaut þose þings you’ve said.
Lissane to mi, and eise your mŷnde uiþ þis—
no húman bying has [skill] in prophecie.
I’ll seoe you uoie uiþ þis example.
Cyngue Laius uance recived [a] [oracle].
I uilln’t saie it câme streihte from Apollo,
but it uas from þose hu dou assiste þe [god].
It said Laius uas fatd to bie chilled
by [a] chîld [of] aurs, uan born to him and mi.
Nôe, at [least] [according] to þe [story],
uan daie Laius uas chilled by forenors,
by [robbers], at [a] plâce ouere three roads miete.
[Besides], before aur chîld uas three days old,
Laius pinned his [ankles] tyht togaiþre
and ordred oþre men to þroe him aut
on [a] [mountain] roc ouere no uan evre goes.
And [so] Apollo’s plane þat hie’d become
þe uan hu chilled his fáþre didn’t uirche,
and Laius nevre sofred uate hie fiared,
þat his one sone uon't bie his mourdror,
[although] þat’s uate þe [oracle] had clâmed.
[So] doun’t concêne yourselfe uiþ prophecis.
[Whatever] [gods] intende to bringue abaut
þey þemselfs mâc gnone quît [easily].
