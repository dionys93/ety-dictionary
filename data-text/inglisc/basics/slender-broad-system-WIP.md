# Inglisc Orthography: The Slender and Broad System

At the heart of the Inglisc spelling reform is the Celtic orthographic concept of "Slender" and "Broad" vowels. In this system, vowels act as the engine of the language—they do not just make their own sounds; they actively radiate influence and dictate the phonetic behavior of every consonant they touch.

By intersecting English voiced/unvoiced consonant pairs with this Slender/Broad mechanic, Inglisc creates a highly consistent, predictable matrix for American English phonetics.

---

## 1. The Vowel Engine (The Triggers)

All vowels in Inglisc are strictly categorized into two polarities. These polarities trigger specific sounds in adjacent consonants. 

### **Slender Vowels**
Slender vowels typically push the tongue toward the hard palate (palatalization), generating softer, fricative, or "y-glide" sounds in nearby consonants.
* **Short:** `e`, `i`
* **Long (Fada):** `é`, `í`

### **Broad Vowels**
Broad vowels typically pull the tongue back toward the soft palate (velarization), keeping adjacent consonants hard, grounded, or guttural.
* **Short:** `a`, `o`, `u`
* **Honorary/Historic:** `y` (serves as the broad /ɪ/ or /ʌ/), `ô` (serves as pushed /ʊ/)
* **Long (Fada):** `á`, `ó`, `ú`
* **Diphthongs:** `ŷ` (/aɪ/), `aṁ`/`oṁ` (/aʊ/)

## 2. The Consonant Matrix (The Reactions)

When a consonant is placed next to a vowel, it looks to that vowel to know how it should be pronounced. 

### **Dental / Alveolar (T & D)**
| Base Letter | Broad Reaction (a, o, u, y, ô) | Slender Reaction (e, i) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **t** | Hard **/t/** *(top)* | Voiceless "th" **/θ/** *(think)* |
| **d** | Hard **/d/** *(dog)* | Voiced "th" **/ð/** *(that)* |

### **Velar / Palatal (C & G)**
| Base Letter | Broad Reaction (a, o, u, y, ô) | Slender Reaction (e, i) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **c** | Hard **/k/** *(cat)* | "ch" **/tʃ/** *(chair)* |
| **g** | Hard **/ɡ/** *(go)* | "y" **/j/** *(yes)* |
| **ġ** | "j" **/dʒ/** *(figure)* | "j" **/dʒ/** *(suggest)* |

## 3. The Mechanics of Spelling

Traditional Irish orthography relies on a strict rule called *caol le caol agus leathan le leathan* (slender with slender, broad with broad), requiring a consonant to be sandwiched by matching vowels on both sides. 

**Inglisc uses a modernized "Relaxed Harmony" rule known as Localized Shielding.**

### **Rule 1: Localized Shielding**
You only insert a silent "shield" vowel when a consonant *must* be protected from an adjacent vowel's influence to preserve the correct English pronunciation. If the phonetic output is already correct, no extra glides are needed.
* **Example:** `embodiment` -> **emboduiment**
  * *Why?* If the `d` touches the slender `i`, it becomes `/ð/` ("th"). We insert the silent broad shield `u` (`-dui-`) to protect the `d`, keeping it a hard English `/d/`.
* **Example:** `kept` -> **keupt**
  * *Why?* The `pt` cluster must remain hard. The `u` shields it from the slender `e`.

### **Rule 2: Consonant Cluster Protection**
A shield vowel protects the *entire* consonant cluster it touches, not just the single adjacent letter.
* **Example:** `steak` -> **staeik**
  * *Why?* The `st` cluster needs to remain hard (/st/). The broad shield (like `u` or `a`) prevents the `s` and `t` from mutating into `/ʃ/` and `/θ/`. 

### **Rule 3: The Old Norse 'K' Loophole**
Because `c` acts as a shapeshifter (becoming `/tʃ/` when slender), we need a way to lock in a hard `/k/` sound next to slender vowels (`e`, `i`). Inglisc adopts the historical Old Norse solution: the letter **`k`**.
* **`C`** is used for hard `/k/` next to *broad* vowels.
* **`K`** is used exclusively to force a hard `/k/` next to *slender* vowels.

## 4. Inglisc in Action

Here is how the Slender/Broad engine translates familiar English words by routing them through the matrix:

* **check -> cek**
  * The initial `c` touches the slender `e` and mutates into `/tʃ/`. The terminal `k` handles the hard `/k/` next to the slender `e`. Three letters, four sounds perfectly coded.
* **kit -> cyt**
  * By utilizing the Welsh `y` as a broad short `/ɪ/` sound, both the `c` and the `t` are kept broad and hard without the need for any silent shields. 
* **cut -> cut**
  * The broad `u` naturally keeps the `c` and `t` hard.
* **keep -> caop**
  * Utilizing the traditional Celtic digraph `ao` (which is grammatically broad but pronounced as a slender long `/iː/`), we can use `c` for the hard `/k/` and bypass the need for a `k` entirely.
* **vision -> bfision**
